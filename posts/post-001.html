One February Afternoon

The sun was hitting the balcony at an angle, slanting in like it had arrived late and hoped nobody would notice. Two chairs sat across from each other, their paint chipped at the arms where years of hands had rested without thinking. Between them, just inside the open door, the stereo hummed softly to itself, as if unsure whether it was being listened to. Sometimes it carried the low, indistinguishable growl of death metal from another lifetime, sometimes the gentle surrender of soft rock, sometimes the careful ache of a ghazal. The songs arrived and left without announcement, swinging between the tastes of two wildly different people.

Nikhil sat with a book of poems in his hand, holding it slightly below his face and looking at it through the lower half of his bifocals. Every now and then, he would lower the book to his lap and lift his coffee, now no longer steaming, and take a slow sip. His white T-shirt bore, just above his stomach, a faint yellow stain he had been trying to hide from his wife. He leaned back, his eyes drifting to the road below, watching nothing in particular, just the occasional passing scooter, the shadow of a passing cloud, and exhaled in a way that suggested he had expected something else to be there.

Across from him, Ananya sat with her own coffee, her blue T-shirt loose around her shoulders, its small white flowers faded unevenly from years of washing. Her hair, still damp from her bath, clung to her neck and temples. Every so often, she would tilt her head slightly or run her fingers through the ends, letting the sun finish what she had begun. One leg rested against the balcony sill, not fully stretched, but placed there out of familiarity. She hummed now and then, almost unconsciously, when the stereo passed through a song she knew. When the heavier songs came on, she would glance briefly toward it, then toward Nikhil, watching him nod faintly along to sounds she could never quite follow, and say nothing.

He turned a page.
The paper made a soft sound, like something settling.

After a while, he cleared his throat—not to announce anything, but because the act of reading sometimes demanded it. He adjusted his glasses, found his place again, and spoke without looking up.

"Scent of Irises, by D.H. Lawrence.
A faint, sickening scent of irises
Persists all morning. Here in a jar on the table
A fine proud spike of purple irises
Rising above the class-room litter, makes me unable
To see the class’s lifted and bended faces
Save in a broken pattern, amid purple and gold and sable.
I can smell the gorgeous bog-end, in its breathless
Dazzle of may-blobs, when the marigold glare overcast you
With fire on your cheeks and your brow and your chin as you dipped
Your face in the marigold bunch, to touch and contrast you,
Your own dark mouth with the bridal faint lady-smocks,
Dissolved on the golden sorcery you should not outlast.
You amid the bog-end’s yellow incantation,
You sitting in the cowslips of the meadow above,
Me, your shadow on the bog-flame, flowery may-blobs,
Me full length in the cowslips, muttering you love;
You, your soul like a lady-smock, lost, evanescent,
You with your face all rich, like the sheen of a dove.
You are always asking, do I remember, remember
The butter-cup bog-end where the flowers rose up
And kindled you over deep with a cast of gold?
You ask again, do the healing days close up
The open darkness which then drew us in,
The dark which then drank up our brimming cup.
You upon the dry, dead beech-leaves, in the fire of night
Burnt like a sacrifice; you invisible;
Only the fire of darkness, and the scent of you!
—And yes, thank God, it still is possible
The healing days shall close the darkness up
Wherein we fainted like a smoke or dew.
Like vapour, dew, or poison. Now, thank God,
The fire of night is gone, and your face is ash
Indistinguishable on the grey, chill day;
The night had burst us out, at last the good
Dark fire burns on untroubled, without clash
Of you upon the dead leaves saying me Yea."

When he finished, he did not look at her immediately. He marked the page with his thumb and reached again for his coffee. Then, slightly tilting his head, he looked at her from over the glasses with a curious face that Ananya did not read.

Ananya did not comment. But the humming stopped. The spring afternoon strolled along the balcony, warming their blankets and drying Ananya's hair. Ananya had taken out her phone and was reading something in it. Casually, she remarked, "Our batchmate, Rohit, became a grandfather today, it seems"

Nikhil replied, not looking up from his book, "Hm, boy or girl?"

"Don't know."
"I see. Good for him."

For a moment, Nikhil left his chair to go get some oranges. As he went past his wife, she gently touched his hand and pointed at her coffee mug. Nikhil bent over and picked it up from the floor where she had kept it and went away. Moments later, he returned, two oranges in his hand. Drawing his chair closer to his wife, he sat down, peeled the first orange and offered it to his wife. She took it, unsatisfied with his peeling, and removed some more of the pith before putting it into her mouth. After a few seconds, once the orange was gone from her mouth, she spat the seeds out and giggled. Nikhil followed suit, having had some oranges himself, and offered another one to Ananya. She inspected it for pith, and satisfied, put the slice into her mouth.

The orange peels lay on the side table by Nikhil's chair. Having satisfied his urge for oranges, he had picked up his book again and was engrossed in it. Poetry was a far more modern acquaintance of Nikhil in the world of literature. All his life, he had mostly read prose, but since his retirement, he felt a profound longing for poetry to understand himself. As if the long, drawn, dramatic sentiments bored him, and the quick burning, fiery, young emotions of the poetry reminded him of what he seemed to miss.

A moment later, he placed the book down and reached into his pocket, pulling out his phone. He held it low, near his lap, angling the screen away from the sun. His thumb hovered there for a second, then pressed once. He watched the screen, his face unchanged, then locked it and slipped it back into his pocket.

The stereo moved on to another song.

Neither of them noticed when the doorbell rang at first. It folded into the afternoon without urgency, a sound like any other.

Nikhil looked up, not startled, but attentive.
He turned his head slightly toward the door, then toward Ananya.
“Could you get that, please, honey?” he said.
She looked at him, mildly puzzled, then set her phone down on her chair and rose without question. He watched her disappear inside, listening to her slow footsteps against the floor.
He did not move.
A moment passed. Then another.
When she returned, she was holding a small parcel wrapped in brown paper, its corners folded with unnecessary care.
She stopped just short of her chair.
“This came for you,” she said, holding it out.
He looked at it, then up at her.
“For us,” he said.
She ran his fingers lightly along the edge of the paper, not opening it.
“What is this for?” she asked.
“Happy Anniversary”, he said, not looking up from his book.
She frowned slightly.
“Our anniversary is in June.”
He shook his head.
“That’s when we got married,” he said. “This is when you first loved me.”

She looked at him, amazed that he remembered, for when they had got married, she had started treating that day as the only anniversary they needed to celebrate. But this man, who forgot what he had come to do in a room after entering it, had somehow remembered that spring evening spent at a university campus.

He met her gaze calmly.
“I have always been the more romantic one, dear,” he added.
She let out a small breath through her nose, something just short of a laugh, and sat back down.
The parcel remained unopened in her hands.

Behind them, the stereo hummed a song, a rare one that both of them liked. It was one of Elvis Presley's quite famous songs, "I can't help/ Falling in love, with you...."
